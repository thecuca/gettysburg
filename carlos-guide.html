For the Curious Traveler

A Short History of Gettysburg

What you're walking through, and why it still matters

01
The Town
Before the Battle

A Crossroads, Not a Target

In the summer of 1863, Gettysburg was a quiet market town of about 2,400 people sitting at the intersection of ten roads. Nobody planned to fight there. The battle began almost by accident on July 1st when Confederate forces looking for a supply of shoes stumbled into Union cavalry on the Chambersburg Pike. Within hours, both armies were funneling tens of thousands of men toward this sleepy Pennsylvania crossroads. The town had no say in what was about to happen to it. Residents hid in cellars, watched from windows, and woke up the next morning to find their streets, homes, churches, and barns turned into a battlefield — and then, for months afterward, into a vast hospital.

2,400 residents · 10 roads converging · ~165,000 soldiers arrived in 3 days
02
The Battle
July 1–3, 1863

Three Days That Turned the War

The fighting lasted three days. On Day One (July 1), the Union was pushed back through town and up onto the high ground south of it — Cemetery Hill and Culp's Hill. On Day Two (July 2), Confederate General Longstreet attacked both flanks. The most famous moment was at Little Round Top, where Colonel Joshua Chamberlain and the 20th Maine — out of ammunition — fixed bayonets and charged downhill, holding the Union's far left and possibly saving the entire army. On Day Three (July 3), General Lee ordered what became known as Pickett's Charge: roughly 12,500 Confederate soldiers marching a mile across open ground into concentrated Union fire. It broke. The Confederate army never invaded the North again. Gettysburg was the turning point — not the end of the war, but the moment the outcome became clear.

~51,000 total casualties across both sides · the deadliest battle ever fought in North America
03
The Fishhook
Reading the Landscape

How to See the Battlefield

The Union line ran roughly in the shape of a fishhook — a curved top anchored at Cemetery Hill and Culp's Hill (the "Eye"), running south along Cemetery Ridge, and hooking sharply at Little Round Top (the "Barb") at the southern end. This shape was actually a strategic advantage: it was compact enough for the Union to shift troops quickly along interior lines, while the Confederates had to march much farther around the outside to coordinate attacks. When you stand on Little Round Top on Wednesday, you can see this entire geometry. The open ground where Pickett's men crossed on Day Three is visible to your north. Devil's Den — the boulder field where Confederate sharpshooters sheltered — is just below you. The whole terrible logic of the battle becomes visible from that one hilltop.

Tuesday = the Eye (Cemetery). Wednesday = the Barb (Little Round Top). Walk both and the battle makes sense.
04
The Civilians
The Town's Experience

What the Walk Is Really About

The "Civilian Experience Walk" on Tuesday isn't just a stroll — it's a way of seeing the battle from the perspective of the 2,400 people who didn't get to leave. Jennie Wade, whose house you'll pass, was the only civilian killed in the battle — shot by a stray bullet while making bread for Union soldiers on July 3rd. The Shriver House was occupied by Confederate sharpshooters who used the upper floors as a firing position; the family had fled but the house still bears the scars. The "Witness Trees" — the ones old enough to have actually been alive in 1863 — are marked throughout town. Every building you eat in, drink in, or walk past was almost certainly pressed into service as a hospital. The smell reportedly lingered for months.

Only one civilian death recorded · every public building became a hospital · recovery took years
05
The Address
November 19, 1863

272 Words That Redefined Everything

Four and a half months after the battle, Lincoln came to Gettysburg to dedicate the new Soldiers' National Cemetery — the ground you'll stand on at sunset Tuesday. He was not the main speaker. That was Edward Everett, a former senator and celebrated orator who spoke for two hours covering the battle in exhaustive detail. Lincoln followed with a speech of 272 words that took roughly two minutes to deliver. The crowd applauded politely but there was no great reaction on the day. Everett himself wrote to Lincoln afterward: "I should be glad if I could flatter myself that I came as near to the central idea of the occasion in two hours as you did in two minutes." What Lincoln did in those two minutes was quietly radical: he reframed the war not as a fight to preserve the Union, but as a test of whether a democratic republic could survive at all — and he redefined the founding of the country to include the promise of equality. It is the most important short speech in American history.

272 words · ~2 minutes · 5 known manuscript drafts · Everett's 2-hour speech is forgotten
06
The Dobbin House
Where You'll Eat Monday

The Oldest Building in Gettysburg

The Dobbin House was built in 1776 — the same year the Declaration of Independence was signed — by Reverend Alexander Dobbin, a Presbyterian minister and schoolteacher who came from Ireland. It predates the town of Gettysburg itself. Before the Civil War it was a stop on the Underground Railroad; there is a hidden chamber beneath the house where freedom seekers are believed to have sheltered. During the battle and in the months that followed, like virtually every large structure in town, it was converted into a field hospital. When you sit down for dinner Monday evening, you are eating in a building that has been standing for 250 years and has seen rather more history than most.

Built 1776 · Underground Railroad stop · field hospital 1863 · oldest structure in Gettysburg
07
The Cyclorama
Wednesday Morning

A 360° Painting of the Decisive Moment

The Cyclorama is a circular oil painting 377 feet in circumference and 42 feet tall, depicting Pickett's Charge at the moment the Confederate line reached the Union center — the "High Water Mark of the Confederacy." It was painted by French artist Paul Philippoteaux between 1882 and 1884, based on interviews with veterans and careful study of the terrain. Cycloramas were the IMAX of the 19th century — immersive, spectacular, designed to make the viewer feel present at the event. This one was exhibited commercially in Boston and Chicago before ending up in Gettysburg. Standing at the center of it, you can see the entire geography of the assault in one sweep — the open ground, the stone wall, the trees — and understand exactly what those men walked into.

377 ft circumference · 42 ft tall · painted 1882–84 · based on veteran interviews and site surveys
